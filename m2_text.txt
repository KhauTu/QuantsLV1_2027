TYPES OF RETURNS FOR FINANCIAL ASSETS, 
INSTRUMENTS, AND INDICATORS
calculate, compare, and interpret different types of returns for 
financial assets, instruments, and indicators
Investors can earn returns from two main sources: uncertain price returns, such as 
price appreciation and price depreciation; and capital distribution returns, which can 
either be fixed or variable. These different returns influence the valuation of financial 
assets and how they are included in investment portfolios.
Types of Returns
The proportion of total return coming from each source varies depending on the 
specific type of financial asset or instrument:
 ■ Price return  is generally uncertain and variable, reflects changes in a finan -
cial asset’s price, and refers to changes in the asset price that are indepen -
dent of the capital income distributions. For equities, it reflects the compa -
ny’s performance, market sentiment, economic and fiscal conditions, and 
other factors. For bonds, it usually reflects the level of interest rates and the 
issuer’s financial health. Many financial assets and instruments generate 
returns primarily through price changes; thus, variability in price changes 
tends to be a strong focus of risk analysis.
 ■ Fixed capital distribution return  is the most common type of return for 
debt. Typically, this rate of return remains unchanged over the investment 
period. The realized and the actual value may vary, for example, in the case 
of issuer default.
 ■ Variable capital distribution return  is the most common type of return 
for equity in the form of dividends and for certain types of debt where 
the coupon rates and payments are variable. Both the rate of return and 
income vary over time, with dividends and floating-rate bonds being typical 
examples.

Types of Returns: Financial Assets
The main financial assets are cash, equity, and debt. There are also hybrids that com -
bine the characteristics of equity and debt.
 ■ Cash, essentially coins, bank notes, currency, and non-interest-bearing bank 
deposits, does not generate any return unless invested. Having cash available 
is vital in finance, investment, and portfolio management; every investment 
will eventually find itself converted back into cash. When cash is invested 
over the short-term, the typical choice is short-term bonds or interest-bear -
ing bank accounts because they earn capital distributions. Long-term invest -
ments include equities, debt, and alternative assets. Those investments earn 
price returns, capital distributions, or typically both.
 ■ Equities, also called stocks, shares, or ownership interests, generate return 
from price appreciation and capital distributions in the form of dividends. 
For Ørsted A/S equity, the components of price and capital distributions 
return between 2017 and 2023 are depicted in Exhibit 1.
 ● Price return . Equity values are determined by a complex interplay of 
company-specific fundamental factors, broader economic indicators, 
market sentiment, and global events, which will be covered in greater 
detail later in the curriculum. As these factors change, equity prices 
and returns change as well. Typically, most returns come from capital 
appreciation.
 ● Capital distributions . Investors who invest capital in corporations may 
receive discretionary capital distributions in the form of dividends or 
other cash payments. Investors receive these discretionary distribu -
tions on a quarterly, semiannual, or annual basis (and there may also be 
special one-off dividends tied to corporate actions). Some companies, 
particularly rapidly growing companies, may choose not to distribute 
dividends and instead reinvest profits to fuel growth. Other compa -
nies distribute a substantial portion of their earnings to shareholders. 
Consequently, investors can select companies that offer the type of 
return they desire.

 ■ Bonds, also called debt, loans, or fixed-income instruments, are contrac -
tual agreements where an investor lends a specific amount of money, the 
principal, to a borrower or an issuer in exchange for regular, predetermined, 
contractual interest or coupon payments. When the bond matures, the 
investor’s principal is typically paid out at maturity, unless the bond is an 
amortizing bond where principal is repaid in regularly scheduled incre -
ments during the maturity of the bond. The issuer may be a corporation, 
government, or other organization. In addition to the capital distributions 
in the form of interest payments, bond investors may also earn returns from 
capital appreciation. For the Ørsted A/S EUR750 million Senior Unsecured 
Bond, the components of price and capital distributions return between 
2018 and 2023 are depicted in Exhibit 2.
 ● Price return . The value of a bond is influenced by numerous factors, 
including the creditworthiness of the borrower, prevailing interest rates, 
and the maturity of the bond. Debt issued by governments, such as gov -
ernment bonds like US Treasuries, are generally considered to have low 
default risk, while corporate bonds can range from low to high default 
risk depending on the issuing company’s financial health. Higher default 
risk typically leads to higher yields to compensate investors for accepting 
the risk. In terms of price return related to rates, generally, longer-dated 
bonds will have more price variability due to rate movements in compar -
ison to shorter-term bonds. Increases in interest rates reduce the value 
of fixed-income streams, while reductions in interest rates increase their 
value.
 ● Capital distributions . Because bond investors regularly receive contrac -
tual interest payments, these investments are particularly attractive to 
investors seeking steady cash income streams. Though not the case in 
this example, it is not atypical for the return earned by bond investors to 
be mainly driven by capital distributions—particularly for shorter-term 
bonds, which are less sensitive to changes in interest rates.

 ■ Hybrids, financial assets that blend equity and debt characteristics, repre -

convertible bonds. Their returns are a middle ground between the fixed

the fixed-income features of bonds with the potentially higher rewards of

Types of Returns: Financial Instruments
 ■ Financial instruments are standardized legal agreements for financial assets, 
such as equity and bonds, as well as derivatives and securitized debt. The 
standardization facilitates public trading among investors. The return 
combines price return and return on capital, and the specific contribution 
of each to the total return depends on the type of financial instrument in 
question. The earlier examples of Ørsted A/S equity and bond returns are 
examples of cash instruments.
 ■ Derivatives are financial instruments whose value is derived from the per -
formance of another, underlying, financial asset or financial indicator, such 
as exchange rate, interest rate, or market index. The value of a derivative is 
based on the expected future price movements of the underlying asset or 
financial indicator. An example of a derivative is a call option to purchase 
stock at a given price on a particular future date.

Types of Returns: Financial Indicators
Financial indicators are integral to the operation of financial markets. Two of the 
most prominent indicators are interest rates and foreign exchange rates. Returns on 
financial assets and instruments are often assessed in relation to different financial 
indicators. Additionally, unlike financial assets and financial instruments, financial 
indicators do not directly generate cash flows.
 ■ Interest rates are typically traded through risk-free, government bonds 
whose prices mirror the prevailing interest rate environment. The inter -
est rate varies inversely with the value of bonds, and thus interest rates 
are inferred from bond prices. Interest rates, as a key economic indicator, 
influence the valuation and performance of various financial instruments, 
including bonds. While the interest rates don’t produce cash flows on their 
own, their level affects the actual returns bond investors earn through capi -
tal gains and capital distributions.
 ■ The risk-free rate  is determined by the return on short-term, risk-free, gov -
ernment debt, as we discuss in the following case study.

Long-Term Asset Return Components
The proportion of returns from capital appreciation and capital distributions has 
shifted over the decades. In the United States during the 1940s, 1950s, 1980s, and 
1990s, equity total returns were primarily driven by capital appreciation. In contrast, 
other decades saw more modest total returns, primarily fueled by capital distribution 
returns. Capital appreciation returns have shown greater volatility compared to capital 
distribution returns.
It is worth noting that dividend distributions have tended downward over time, 
reflecting a shift in profit distribution strategies by large US corporations, which were 
potentially influenced by a 1982 regulatory change that provided a safe harbor from 
market manipulation charges. Increasingly, corporations have opted to repurchase their 
own shares rather than issue cash dividends. Share buybacks reduce the number of

Risks and Returns
Risk stems from the uncertainty between the expected returns on an investment and 
the actual, realized returns. This risk can reflect either pervasive macro factors or 
issuer-specific micro factors.
 ■ Macro risks or systematic risks  are broad economy-wide risks that influ -
ence the performance of investments. The returns are systematically depen -
dent on these factors. If the economy or the financial markets perform 
well, asset returns are likely to follow suit. Systematic risk largely cannot be 
mitigated and thus is also called non-diversifiable risk.
 ■ Micro risks or unsystematic risks  are associated with factors specific to 
the issuer and determine investment returns based on the firm’s underlying 
economic characteristics, such as earning power, market position, finan -
cial strength captured by its sales and sales growth, operating margins, and 
return on assets and equity. Unsystematic risks can be mitigated through 
diversification; they are diversifiable risks.
Three interrelated systematic risk factors are common to all assets: inflation, 
interest rates, and market movements.
 ■ Inflation risk is the risk that investment returns will deviate from expecta -
tions solely due to effects of inflation (or deflation). The primary investment 
objective is to earn a positive real rate of return, making inflation risk a 
major concern for portfolio managers and clients globally.
 ■ Interest rate risk is the uncertainty of returns due to interest rate fluctu -
ations. Asset prices generally rise with falling interest rates and vice versa. 
This inverse relationship is pervasive, though it affects different investments 
to varying extents. Generally speaking, the value of any investment depends 
on its future cash flows. Those future cash flows are worth more when inter -
est rates are lower than they would be if interest rates were higher.
 ■ Market risk represents the possibility that market forces will affect the 
returns of all financial assets in an unexpected manner.
Unsystematic risks for an investment include issuer risk and sector or industry risk.
 ■ Issuer risk, or specific risk, encompasses a firm’s business and financial 
risks. Business risk is inherent in the nature of the firm’s operations, while 
financial risk arises from the use of financial leverage. Credit risk relates to 
a firm’s ability to meet its payment promises: in case of debt, repayment of 
debt, and periodic interest payments.

 ■ Sector risks or industry risks  are similar to market risk in that they repre -
sent the possibility that forces particular to a sector will affect the returns 
of all assets within that grouping in an unexpected manner. The investment 
environment often necessitates a simplified sector classification for practical 
portfolio management, such as sector rotation strategies.
There are many risk measures to help us quantify total risk and its components, 
and these will be explored in later sections. One straightforward approach we address 
in this lesson is to calculate the range between the highest and lowest returns and 
relate this range to the average return, as shown in Exhibit 10.
The range of annual returns represents the variability of these returns, indicating 
the uncertainty of returns and the potential risk faced by investors. A narrower range 
suggests that returns are relatively consistent and predictable, indicating lower uncer-
tainty about their size and direction—in other words, lower risk. A relatively wide 
range signifies significant variability in annual returns across time, making returns 
less predictable. Such variability indicates unpredictability and implies higher risk, 
including greater potential losses as well as greater potential gains.
The higher risk premium of riskier investments compensates investors for taking 
on greater volatility and uncertainty, offering the potential for higher returns. This 
premium represents the additional expected return for taking on higher risk. It varies 
depending on the financial asset: bonds or equity, investment time horizon (short 
or long term), and other specific risk factors. Consequently, conservative investors 
prioritize stability and predictability, choosing investments with lower risk premiums 
and more modest potential returns.
