\section{TIME VALUE OF MONEY IN FIXED INCOME AND EQUITY}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{0.06\linewidth} | p{0.88\linewidth}}
	a & calculate and interpret the present value of fixed-income and equity instruments based on expected future cash flows \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The relationship between a current or present value, $PV_0$, and future value, $FV_t$, of a cash flow—where $r$ is the stated discount rate per period and $t$ is the number of compounding periods—is:
\begin{equation}
	FV_t = PV_0 (1 + r)^t
	\label{eq:fv_simple_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 137

If the compounding frequency approaches infinity (continuous compounding), we calculate the future value as follows:
\begin{equation}
	FV_t = PV_0 e^{r \times t}
	\label{eq:fv_continuous_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 138

Conversely, we can solve for the present value of a future cash flow under simple and continuous compounding:
\begin{equation}
	PV_0 = FV_t (1 + r)^{-t} = \frac{FV_t}{(1 + r)^t}
	\label{eq:pv_simple_m4}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
	PV_0 = FV_t e^{-r \times t}
	\label{eq:pv_continuous_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 138

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Case Study: Annualizing Returns across Different Horizons},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
Annualized returns provide a standardized framework for comparing investments with different holding periods:
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{annualized}} = (1 + r_{\text{periodic}})^{\frac{365}{\text{Days}}} - 1
	\label{eq:annualize_returns_m4}
\end{equation}
For three securities in a portfolio held for different durations:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Security A:} Earned $6.2\%$ over 100 days. Annualized return:
	      \[ r_{\text{annual}} = (1 + 0.062)^{365/100} - 1 \approx 24.55\% \]
	\item \textbf{Security B:} Earned $2.0\%$ over four weeks (28 days). Annualized return:
	      \[ r_{\text{annual}} = (1 + 0.02)^{52/4} - 1 \approx 29.36\% \]
	\item \textbf{Security C:} Earned $5.0\%$ over three months (90 days). Annualized return:
	      \[ r_{\text{annual}} = (1 + 0.05)^{365/90} - 1 \approx 21.55\% \]
\end{itemize}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Quy đổi lợi nhuận về cùng năm (annualization) giúp chúng ta so sánh các kỳ hạn khách quan. Ở đây, Security B tuy suất sinh lời thấp nhất ($2.0\%$) nhưng hiệu quả nhất năm ($29.36\%$) nhờ nắm giữ ngắn (4 tuần).}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Fixed-Income Instruments and the Time Value of Money}

Cash flows associated with fixed-income instruments (debt) usually follow one of three general patterns:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Discount (Zero-Coupon) Bonds:} Single payment representing both interest and principal repayment at maturity.
	\item \textbf{Coupon Bonds:} Regular periodic interest coupon payments with the principal repaid at maturity.
	\item \textbf{Annuities / Fully Amortizing Loans:} Equal periodic payments containing both interest and principal over the life of the instrument.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Discount Instruments}
The cash flow pattern of a discount bond is illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_1}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/004_Module_4/exhibit_1.png}
	\caption{Discount Bond Cash Flows}
	\label{fig:mod4_exhibit_1}
\end{figure}

The present value calculation for a discount bond with principal $FV_t$ repaid fully after $t$ periods with a periodic market discount rate of $r$ per period is:
\begin{equation}
	PV_{\text{Discount}} = \frac{FV_t}{(1 + r)^t}
	\label{eq:pv_discount_bond_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 140

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Case Study: Discount Bonds under Positive and Negative Interest Rates},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Positive Rates (India STRIPS):} A 20-year stripped government bond with $FV = \text{INR } 100$ and required return $r = 6.70\%$:
	      \[ PV_0 = \frac{\text{INR } 100}{(1 + 0.067)^{20}} \approx \text{INR } 27.33 \]
	      If rates remain unchanged, after three years ($t = 17$ remaining):
	      \[ PV_3 = \frac{\text{INR } 100}{(1 + 0.067)^{17}} \approx \text{INR } 33.21 \]
	      If rates immediately rise to $7.70\%$ at $t = 0$:
	      \[ PV_0 = \frac{\text{INR } 100}{(1 + 0.077)^{20}} \approx \text{INR } 22.68 \quad (\text{reflects the inverse price-yield relationship}) \]
	
	\item \textbf{Negative Rates (German Bund):} A 10-year sovereign bond with $FV = \text{EUR } 100$ issued at a negative yield $r = -0.05\%$:
	      \[ PV_0 = \frac{\text{EUR } 100}{(1 - 0.0005)^{10}} \approx \text{EUR } 100.50 \]
\end{enumerate}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Khi lãi suất dương, giá trái phiếu chiết khấu tăng dần (accretes) về mệnh giá. Khi lãi suất âm, nhà đầu tư trả phí giữ vốn an toàn, khiến giá trái phiếu giảm dần (amortizes) về mệnh giá tại ngày đáo hạn.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Coupon Instruments}
The cash flow pattern of a coupon bond is illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_2}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/004_Module_4/exhibit_2.png}
	\caption{Coupon Bond Cash Flows}
	\label{fig:mod4_exhibit_2}
\end{figure}

The price of a coupon bond is the sum of the present values of all periodic coupon payments and the present value of the final principal repayment:
\begin{equation}
	PV_{\text{Coupon}} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{PMT}{(1 + r)^t} + \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^T}
	\label{eq:pv_coupon_bond_base_m4}
\end{equation}
Using the sum of a geometric series, this simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
	PV_{\text{Coupon}} = \frac{PMT}{r} \left[ 1 - \frac{1}{(1 + r)^T} \right] + \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^T}
	\label{eq:pv_coupon_bond_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 143

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Case Study: Coupon Pricing and Compounding Frequencies},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Annual Coupons (Greece):} A 7-year bond with a $2\%$ annual coupon ($FV = \text{EUR } 100$) and $r = 2.00\%$ yield:
	      \[ PV = \frac{\text{EUR } 2}{0.02} \left[ 1 - \frac{1}{(1.02)^7} \right] + \frac{\text{EUR } 100}{(1.02)^7} = \text{EUR } 100 \quad (\text{Par Bond}) \]
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Coupon Rate = Yield $\implies PV = FV$ (Par Bond)
		      \item Coupon Rate > Yield $\implies PV > FV$ (Premium Bond)
		      \item Coupon Rate < Yield $\implies PV < FV$ (Discount Bond)
	      \end{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Semiannual Coupons (India):} A 20-year bond with a $6.70\%$ coupon ($FV = \text{INR } 100$) paid semiannually. Periodic coupon $PMT = \text{INR } 3.35$, periodic discount rate $r = 3.35\%$, total periods $T = 40$:
	      \[ PV = \frac{3.35}{0.0335} \left[ 1 - \frac{1}{1.0335^{40}} \right] + \frac{100}{1.0335^{40}} = \text{INR } 100 \]
	      If the yield-to-maturity immediately rises to $7.70\%$ (periodic rate $r = 3.85\%$):
	      \[ PV = \frac{3.35}{0.0385} \left[ 1 - \frac{1}{1.0385^{40}} \right] + \frac{100}{1.0385^{40}} \approx \text{INR } 89.88 \]
\end{enumerate}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Khi định giá trái phiếu, tần suất ghép lãi rất quan trọng. Khoản INR 100 ở năm 20: ghép năm có PV = INR 27.33, ghép nửa năm (40 kỳ) có PV = INR 26.77. Ghép lãi thường xuyên hơn làm giảm PV.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Floating-Rate and Perpetual Bonds}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Floating-Rate Bonds:} Adjust their coupon payments periodically based on a reference rate (e.g., NIBOR, SOFR) plus a fixed credit margin.
	\item \textbf{Perpetual Bonds:} Have no stated maturity date ($T \to \infty$), paying coupons indefinitely. The valuation simplifies to:
	      \begin{equation}
		      PV_{\text{Perpetual}} = \frac{PMT}{r}
		      \label{eq:pv_perpetual_bond_m4}
	      \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 146--147

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Examples: Floating-Rate and Perpetual Valuations},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Floating-Rate (Sogn og Fjordane Energi A/S):} Norwegian hydropower green bond with quarterly coupon. Coupon Rate = 3-month NIBOR + 73 bps margin. If NIBOR is $4.52\%$, the quarterly periodic rate is:
	      \[ r_{\text{periodic}} = \frac{4.52\% + 0.73\%}{4} = 1.3125\% \]
	      The periodic coupon payment for a $\text{NOK } 1 \text{ million}$ face value is:
	      \[ PMT = \text{NOK } 1,000,000 \times 1.3125\% = \text{NOK } 13,125 \]
	\item \textbf{Perpetual Bond (KB Financial):} Perpetual debt with a $3.30\%$ coupon paid quarterly ($0.825\%$ periodic coupon). If the required return is $3.40\%$ quarterly compounded (periodic rate $0.85\%$):
	      \[ PV = \frac{\text{KRW } 8.25}{0.0085} \approx \text{KRW } 970.59 \quad (\text{per KRW 1,000 face value}) \]
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Annuities and Fully Amortizing Loans}
The cash flow pattern for fully amortizing loans (mortgages) is illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_3}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/004_Module_4/exhibit_3.png}
	\caption{Mortgage Cash Flows}
	\label{fig:mod4_exhibit_3}
\end{figure}

For level-payment instruments (like mortgages), the periodic level cash flow, $PMT$, containing both interest and principal repayment is:
\begin{equation}
	PMT = \frac{r \times PV_0}{1 - (1 + r)^{-t}}
	\label{eq:pmt_annuity_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 148

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Case Study: Mortgage Cash Flow Amortization},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
A 30-year residential mortgage is used to finance $80\%$ of a $\text{USD } 1,000,000$ building. Loan amount $PV_0 = \text{USD } 800,000$. Mortgage rate is $5.25\%$ annual monthly compounded ($r = 0.4375\%$ per month, $t = 360$):
\[ PMT = \frac{0.004375 \times \text{USD } 800,000}{1 - (1.004375)^{-360}} \approx \text{USD } 4,417.63 \text{ per month} \]
The amortization breakdown for the first two months is:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Month 1:}
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Interest payment: $\text{USD } 800,000 \times 0.4375\% = \text{USD } 3,500.00$
		      \item Principal repayment: $\text{USD } 4,417.63 - \text{USD } 3,500.00 = \text{USD } 917.63$
		      \item Remaining balance: $\text{USD } 800,000 - \text{USD } 917.63 = \text{USD } 799,082.37$
	      \end{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Month 2:}
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Interest payment: $\text{USD } 799,082.37 \times 0.4375\% = \text{USD } 3,495.99$
		      \item Principal repayment: $\text{USD } 4,417.63 - \text{USD } 3,495.99 = \text{USD } 921.64$
		      \item Remaining balance: $\text{USD } 799,082.37 - \text{USD } 921.64 = \text{USD } 798,160.73$
	      \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Dư nợ giảm dần: Dù tổng tiền trả hàng tháng cố định ở USD 4,417.63, cấu phần lãi giảm dần do dư nợ gốc giảm, và cấu phần gốc tăng dần.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{EQUITY INSTRUMENTS AND THE TIME VALUE OF MONEY}

Equity shares have no maturity date and are assumed to remain outstanding indefinitely. Share valuation is based on discounting expected discretionary cash dividends.

\subsection{Constant Dividends}
The cash flow pattern for equity paying constant dividends is illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_5}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/004_Module_4/exhibit_5.png}
	\caption{Equity Cash Flows with Constant Dividends}
	\label{fig:mod4_exhibit_5}
\end{figure}

The valuation of equity paying constant dividends in perpetuity simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
	PV_t = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \frac{Div_{t+1}}{(1 + r)^i} = \frac{Div_{t+1}}{r}
	\label{eq:pv_preferred_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 152

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Example: Shipline PLC Constant Dividend Valuation},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
Shipline PLC pays a constant annual dividend of $\text{GBP } 1.50$ indefinitely. With a required return $r = 15\%$:
\[ PV_0 = \frac{\text{GBP } 1.50}{0.15} = \text{GBP } 10.00 \]
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Constant Dividend Growth (Gordon Growth Model)}
The cash flow pattern for equity paying constant growth dividends is illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_6}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/004_Module_4/exhibit_6.png}
	\caption{Equity Cash Flows with Constant Dividend Growth}
	\label{fig:mod4_exhibit_6}
\end{figure}

If dividends grow at a constant rate $g$ in perpetuity:
\begin{equation}
	PV_t = \frac{Div_t (1 + g)}{r - g} = \frac{Div_{t+1}}{r - g}
	\label{eq:gordon_growth_m4}
\end{equation}
where $r - g > 0$.
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 153

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Example: Shipline PLC Constant Growth Valuation},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
If Shipline PLC’s dividend grows at a constant $g = 6\%$ indefinitely, next year's dividend is:
\[ Div_1 = \text{GBP } 1.50 \times (1 + 0.06) = \text{GBP } 1.59 \]
The expected stock price is:
\[ PV_0 = \frac{\text{GBP } 1.59}{0.15 - 0.06} \approx \text{GBP } 17.67 \]

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Chênh lệch $r - g$ ở mẫu số cực kỳ quan trọng. Khi chênh lệch này nhỏ (tốc độ tăng trưởng sát tỷ suất sinh lời yêu cầu), giá cổ phiếu cực kỳ nhạy cảm với bất kỳ thay đổi nhỏ nào của $r$ hoặc $g$.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Two-Stage Dividend Growth Model}
The cash flow pattern of a two-stage dividend growth model is illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_7}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/004_Module_4/exhibit_7.png}
	\caption{Equity Cash Flows with Two-Stage Dividend Growth}
	\label{fig:mod4_exhibit_7}
\end{figure}

For companies experiencing a rapid initial growth phase ($g_s$) for $n$ periods, followed by a stable long-term growth rate ($g_l$):
\begin{equation}
	PV_0 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{Div_0 (1 + g_s)^i}{(1 + r)^i} + \frac{E[S_n]}{(1 + r)^n}
	\label{eq:two_stage_growth_base_m4}
\end{equation}
Where the expected terminal value at the end of the high-growth phase $E[S_n]$ is:
\begin{equation}
	E[S_n] = \frac{Div_{n+1}}{r - g_l}
	\label{eq:terminal_value_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 154

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Example: Shipline PLC Two-Stage Dividend Growth Valuation},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
Assume Shipline grows its dividend by $g_s = 6\%$ for three years, followed by $g_l = 2\%$ constant growth. $r = 15\%$, $Div_0 = \text{GBP } 1.50$:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Step 1: PV of high-growth dividends (first 3 years):}
	      \[ Div_1 = 1.5900 \implies PV(Div_1) = \frac{1.5900}{1.15} \approx 1.3826 \]
	      \[ Div_2 = 1.6854 \implies PV(Div_2) = \frac{1.6854}{1.15^2} \approx 1.2744 \]
	      \[ Div_3 = 1.7865 \implies PV(Div_3) = \frac{1.7865}{1.15^3} \approx 1.1747 \]
	      \[ \text{Sum of PV of high-growth phase} \approx 3.8317 \]
	\item \textbf{Step 2: PV of terminal value at Year 3:}
	      \[ Div_4 = Div_3 \times (1 + g_l) = 1.7865 \times 1.02 \approx \text{GBP } 1.8222 \]
	      \[ Terminal\ Value\ E[S_3] = \frac{1.8222}{0.15 - 0.02} \approx \text{GBP } 14.0169 \]
	      \[ PV\ of\ E[S_3] = \frac{14.0169}{1.15^3} \approx \text{GBP } 9.2163 \]
	\item \textbf{Step 3: Total price:}
	      \[ PV_0 = 3.8317 + 9.2163 = \text{GBP } 13.0480 \approx \text{GBP } 13.05 \]
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{center}

When market prices and expected cash flows are observed, analysts can calculate the implied rate of return and the implied growth rate to assess valuation.

\subsection{Implied Return for Fixed-Income Instruments}
The price-yield relationship of a discount bond and its implied return are illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_8}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/004_Module_4/exhibit_8.png}
	\caption{Discount Bond Implied Return}
	\label{fig:mod4_exhibit_8}
\end{figure}

For discount bonds, the implied return is:
\begin{equation}
	r = \left( \frac{FV_t}{PV_0} \right)^{\frac{1}{t}} - 1
	\label{eq:implied_return_discount_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 150

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Case Studies: Implied returns for zero-coupon and coupon bonds},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Zero-Coupon (German sovereign bond):} 10-year discount bond issued at $\text{EUR } 100.50$ per $\text{EUR } 100$ principal ($r = -0.05\%$). Six years later, it trades at $\text{EUR } 95.72$.
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Annualized holding return for initial buyer (6 years):
		            \[ r_{\text{hold}} = \left( \frac{95.72}{100.50} \right)^{1/6} - 1 \approx -0.81\% \]
		      \item Expected annualized return for buyer at Year 6 (4 years remaining):
		            \[ r_{\text{buy}} = \left( \frac{100}{95.72} \right)^{1/4} - 1 \approx 1.10\% \]
	      \end{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Coupon-Paying (Greece 2\% annual bond):} Issued at par ($\text{EUR } 100$, YTM $2\%$). Two years later, price drops to $\text{EUR } 93.091$.
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Implied YTM for remaining 5 years: Solved iteratively using RATE function:
		            \[ \text{Rate}(5, 2, -93.091, 100) \approx 3.5320\% \]
		      \item Two-year holding return if coupons are reinvested at $2.00\%$:
		            \[ FV_2 = 2(1.02) + 2 + 93.091 = \text{EUR } 97.13 \implies r_{\text{hold}} = \left( \frac{97.13}{100} \right)^{1/2} - 1 \approx -1.445\% \]
	      \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Nhận xét: Ở ví dụ trái phiếu Hy Lạp, khoản lỗ vốn (từ EUR 100 xuống EUR 93.091) lớn hơn coupon tích lũy (EUR 4.04), khiến suất sinh lời thực tế bị âm trong 2 năm đầu dù coupon dương.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Equity Implied Return, Implied Growth, and Forward P/E Ratios}

Rearranging the Gordon growth model, we can solve for implied return ($r$) and implied growth rate ($g$):
\begin{equation}
	r = \frac{Div_{t+1}}{PV_0} + g
	\label{eq:implied_return_equity_m4}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
	g = r - \frac{Div_{t+1}}{PV_0}
	\label{eq:implied_growth_equity_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 166

By dividing the valuation formula by expected earnings per share ($E_{t+1}$), we obtain the forward Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio:
\begin{equation}
	\frac{PV_t}{E_{t+1}} = \frac{Div_{t+1} / E_{t+1}}{r - g}
	\label{eq:forward_pe_m4}
\end{equation}
where $Div_{t+1}/E_{t+1}$ represents the expected dividend payout ratio.
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 168

]
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Coca-Cola Stock:} Trades at $\text{USD } 63.00$, next year expected dividend is $\text{USD } 1.76$.
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item If $g = 4.00\%$ constant growth:
		            \[ r_{\text{implied}} = \frac{1.76}{63.00} + 0.04 \approx 6.79\% \]
		      \item If required return $r = 7.00\%$:
		            \[ g_{\text{implied}} = 0.07 - \frac{1.76}{63.00} \approx 4.21\% \]
	      \end{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Coca-Cola Forward P/E:} Forward P/E is 28, payout ratio is $70\%$.
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item If required return $r = 9.00\%$ and growth $g = 4.50\%$:
		            \[ \text{Valuation P/E} = \frac{0.70}{0.09 - 0.045} \approx 15.56 \]
		            Since the market P/E ($28$) is much higher than the fundamental valuation ($15.56$), the stock is \textbf{overvalued} (recommend short position).
	      \end{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Indian NIFTY 50 Index:} Forward P/E is 15, expected payout ratio is $50\%$, required return $r = 7.50\%$, expected growth $g = 4.50\%$:

\end{enumerate}

\section{CASH FLOW ADDITIVITY}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{0.06\linewidth} | p{0.88\linewidth}}
	c & explain the cash flow additivity principle and its importance for the condition of no arbitrage, and explain its use in calculating implied forward interest rates, forward exchange rates, and option values \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The cash flow additivity principle states that the present value of any sum of future cash flows equals the sum of their individual present values. This principle is vital in maintaining the no-arbitrage condition (meaning it is impossible to make a riskless profit).

The comparison between two investment strategies under this principle is illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_13}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/004_Module_4/exhibit_13.png}
	\caption{Two Investment Strategies}
	\label{fig:mod4_exhibit_13}
\end{figure}

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Case Study: Basic Cash Flow Additivity},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
Consider two strategies with an initial investment of $\text{GBP } 100$ and $r=10\%$:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Strategy 1:} Cash flows are $-100, +45, +45, +45$.
	      \[ PV_1 = -100 + \frac{45}{1.1} + \frac{45}{1.1^2} + \frac{45}{1.1^3} \approx \text{GBP } 11.91 \]
	\item \textbf{Strategy 2:} Cash flows are $-100, +60, +40, +32.35$.
	      \[ PV_2 = -100 + \frac{60}{1.1} + \frac{40}{1.1^2} + \frac{32.35}{1.1^3} \approx \text{GBP } 11.91 \]
	\item \textbf{Incremental Cash Flow Diff:} Strategy 1 -- Strategy 2 cash flows: $0, -15, +5, +12.65$.
	      \[ PV_{\text{Diff}} = -\frac{15}{1.1} + \frac{5}{1.1^2} + \frac{12.65}{1.1^3} \approx 0 \]
\end{itemize}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Tính cộng dồn dòng tiền (additivity): Hai danh mục tạo ra dòng tiền có PV tương đương phải giao dịch cùng giá để loại bỏ cơ hội arbitrage.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Implied Forward Interest Rates Using Cash Flow Additivity}
The relationship between spot rates and implied forward rates is illustrated in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod4_exhibit_15}.

Under the no-arbitrage condition, investing for $k$ periods must yield the same return as investing for $j$ periods ($j < k$) and then reinvesting for the remaining $k-j$ periods at the forward rate:
\begin{equation}
	(1 + S_k)^k = (1 + S_j)^j (1 + F_{j, k-j})^{k-j}
	\label{eq:forward_rate_spot_m4}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 4, Page 175

The forward exchange rate $F$ (expressed as price/base) under continuous compounding interest rate parity is:

	\item \textbf{US/Japan (USD to JPY, 6 months):} Spot JPY/USD = 134.40. $r_{\text{JPY}} = 0.05\%$, $r_{\text{USD}} = 2.00\%$ (continuously compounded):
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Strategy 1 (Domestic USD): $\text{USD } 1,000 \times e^{0.02 \times 0.5} = \text{USD } 1,010.05$
		      \item Strategy 2 (Convert JPY): $\text{USD } 1,000 \to \text{JPY } 134,400$. Grows to $\text{JPY } 134,400 \times e^{0.0005 \times 0.5} \approx \text{JPY } 134,433.60$.

\end{enumerate}

We can calculate the no-arbitrage price of derivative options using a replicating portfolio consisting of a position in the underlying asset ($\Delta$ shares) and risk-free lending/borrowing.

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Case Study: Call and Put Option replication pricing},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
Stock price $S_0 = 40$. In one year, it can go up to $S_1^u = 56$ or down to $S_1^d = 32$. Risk-free rate is $5\%$. Option strike price $X = 50$.

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Call Option ($c_1^u = 6$, $c_1^d = 0$):} Replicate with $\Delta$ stock and short 1 call option.
	      \[ \text{Hedge Ratio } \Delta = \frac{c_1^u - c_1^d}{S_1^u - S_1^d} = \frac{6 - 0}{56 - 32} = 0.25 \]
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Portfolio Value in up state: $0.25 \times 56 - 6 = 8$
		      \item Portfolio Value in down state: $0.25 \times 32 - 0 = 8$
	      \end{itemize}
	      Since the portfolio is risk-free, its initial value must equal the discounted payoff of 8:
	      \[ 0.25(40) - c_0 = \frac{8}{1.05} \approx 7.62 \implies c_0 = 10 - 7.62 = \text{CNY } 2.38 \]
	\item \textbf{Put Option ($p_1^u = 0$, $p_1^d = 18$):} Replicate with $\Delta$ stock and long 1 put option.
	      \[ \text{Hedge Ratio } \Delta = \frac{p_1^d - p_1^u}{S_1^u - S_1^d} = \frac{18 - 0}{56 - 32} = 0.75 \]
	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item Portfolio Value in up state: $0.75 \times 56 + 0 = 42$
		      \item Portfolio Value in down state: $0.75 \times 32 + 18 = 42$
	      \end{itemize}
	      Setting initial value equal to discounted payoff of 42:
	      \[ 0.75(40) + p_0 = \frac{42}{1.05} = 40 \implies p_0 = 40 - 30 = \text{CNY } 10 \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}
