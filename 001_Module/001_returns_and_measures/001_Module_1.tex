\subsection{Total Return}
\begin{equation}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{2.0}
\begin{array}{r @{{}={}} c @{{}+{}} c}
\text{Total Return} & \text{Capital Appreciation Return} & \text{Capital Distribution Return} \\
r & r_{\text{price}} & r_{\text{distribution}} \\
  & \dfrac{P_1-P_0}{P_0} & \dfrac{\text{Inc}}{P_0}
\end{array}
\label{eq:total_return}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 8

For capital distributions on equities, we focus on the dividend yield; for bonds, we look at current yield.
\begin{itemize}
	\item Some expressions of Return:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item[] \textbf{Expected returns} (ex ante returns): are the anticipated returns on an investment over a specified period.
		\item[] \textbf{Actual returns} (ex post returns): are the returns on an investment after the investment period has ended.
		\item[] \textbf{Unrealized returns}: changes in value represent while an investor holds an investment.
		\item[] \textbf{Realized returns}: when the investment is ultimately sold, gains and losses would be realized at the current market value.
	\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Arithmetic and Geometric Holding Period Return}
To ensure accurate comparisons across different investments and time periods, it's crucial that these returns are calculated using a uniform time period.
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{arithmetic mean return}:
	\begin{equation}
		\bar{r}_A = \frac{r_1 + r_2 + \dots + r_T}{T} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}r_t
		\label{eq:arithmetic_mean}
	\end{equation}
	% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 12

	\item \textbf{geometric mean return}:
	\begin{equation}
		\bar{r}_G = \sqrt[T]{(1 + r_1) \times (1 + r_2) \times \dots \times (1 + r_T)} - 1 = \left[ \prod_{t=1}^{T}(1+r_t) \right]^{1/T} - 1
		\label{eq:geometric_mean}
	\end{equation}
	% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 13
\end{itemize}

Geometric mean is a more accurate reflection of investment performance over time.
Arithmetic average return calculations assume that the amount invested at the beginning of the period does not change,
but the base amount changes due to capital appreciation or depreciation.
The arithmetic-geometric mean inequality highlights the impact of volatility on investment returns.

\begin{examtip}
Remember:
- \textbf{Arithmetic Mean} is the best estimator of return for a single future period (ex-ante).
- \textbf{Geometric Mean} represents the actual historical compound rate of return over multiple periods (ex-post).
- Unless all period returns are identical, \textbf{Arithmetic Mean is always strictly greater than Geometric Mean} due to volatility ($ \bar{r}_A > \bar{r}_G $).
\end{examtip}

\subsection{Compounding: Daily, Monthly, Quarterly, and Annual Returns}
With $c$ is the number of compounding periods in a year,
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{annualized}} = (1 + r_{\text{period}})^c - 1
	\label{eq:annualized_return}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 17

One major limitation of annualizing returns is the implicit assumption that earned returns can be repeated.
\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Example: Compounding and Annualizing Returns},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
Consider a bank deposit with a 3-month periodic interest rate of $1.50\%$.
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Compounding periods per year:} $c = 4$.
	\item \textbf{Annualized return calculation:}
	      \[ r_{\text{annualized}} = (1 + 0.015)^4 - 1 \approx 6.136\% \]
\end{itemize}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Thực tế: Lãi suất 6.136\% lớn hơn mức 6.0\% nhân đơn ($1.5\% \times 4$), phản ánh lãi kép khi tiền lãi kỳ trước được tái đầu tư sinh lãi kỳ sau.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Continuous Compounding}
The more frequently we compound returns, the higher the annual return will be; however, there is a limit.
As shown in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod1_exhibit_10}:
\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/001_Module_1/exhibit_10.png}
	\caption{The Impact of Compounding}
	\label{fig:mod1_exhibit_10}
\end{figure}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 28

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{continuously compounded return}: 
	\begin{equation}
		\tilde{r}_{t, t+1} = \ln \left( \frac{P_{t+1}}{P_t} \right) = \ln(1 + r_{t, t+1})
		\label{eq:continuous_compounding}
	\end{equation}
	% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 20
\end{itemize}

The additive property of continuously compounded returns provides calculation advantages:
\begin{align*}
	\frac{P_T}{P_0} & = \frac{P_T}{P_{T-1}} \cdot \frac{P_{T-1}}{P_{T-2}} \dots \frac{P_2}{P_1} \cdot \frac{P_1}{P_0} \\
	\Rightarrow \ln\left(\frac{P_T}{P_0}\right) &= \ln\left(\frac{P_T}{P_{T-1}}\right) + \ln\left(\frac{P_{T-1}}{P_{T-2}}\right) + \dots + \ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) + \ln\left(\frac{P_1}{P_0}\right) \\
	\Rightarrow \tilde{r}_{0, T} &=  \tilde{r}_{T-1, T} + \tilde{r}_{T-2, T-1} + \dots + \tilde{r}_{1, 2} + \tilde{r}_{0, 1} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \tilde{r}_{t-1, t}
\end{align*}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Tại sao suất sinh lời ghép lãi liên tục (log return) được ưa chuộng hơn suất sinh lời rời rạc? Nhờ tính cộng gộp thời gian (time-additivity): tổng log return các ngày bằng log return cả năm, giúp đơn giản hóa mô hình toán học.}}

\subsection{The Required Rate of Return}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{opportunity cost}: that incurs when delaying consumption, or the value that investors forgo by choosing a course of action.
	\item \textbf{required rate of return}: the minimum rate of return an investor must receive to accept the investment.
\end{itemize}
Components of Required Rate of Return:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{risk-free rate}: is the return on an investment without any default or reinvestment risk.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item[] It refers to returns on short-term government bonds, such as the 30-day US Treasury bill. The bonds have a short maturity, which reduces their sensitivity to interest rates changes.
		\item[] \textbf{real risk-free rate} reflects the time preferences of individuals for current versus future real consumption.
		\begin{equation}
			r_{\text{real\_rf}} = \frac{1 + r_{\text{rf}}}{1 + \text{infl}} - 1 \quad \text{or quick approximation: } r_{\text{real\_rf}} \approx r_{\text{rf}} - \text{infl}
			\label{eq:real_risk_free}
		\end{equation}
		% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 31
	\end{itemize}
	\item Total required returns for any asset can then be approximated as:
	\begin{equation}
		r = r_{\text{rf}} + \text{infl} + \sum_{j=1}^{n} (f_j \times \text{rp}_j)
		\label{eq:required_return}
	\end{equation}
	% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 31
	\begin{itemize}
		\item[] $n$ is the number of risk factors
		\item[] $f_{j}$ is the exposure of the security to the $j^{\text{th}}$ risk factor
		\item[] $\text{rp}_j$ is the specific risk premium associated with the $j^{\text{th}}$ risk factor
		\item[] $\sum_{j=1}^{n} (f_j \times \text{rp}_j)$ is the sum of all risk premia.
	\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

As shown in Exhibit \ref{fig:mod1_exhibit_13}:
\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{002_figs/001_Module_1/exhibit_13.png}
	\caption{Components of Required Rate of Return and Examples of Different Risk Premia}
	\label{fig:mod1_exhibit_13}
\end{figure}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 31

Examples of risk premia for bonds:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Default risk premium}: the additional return for buying a riskier corporate bond instead of a less-risky government bond.
	\item \textbf{Liquidity premium}: the additional return for holding a bond that may be harder to sell quickly.
	\item \textbf{Maturity risk premium}: the additional return for holding more interest rate sensitive, longer maturity bonds.
\end{itemize}
Examples of risk premia for stocks:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Size risk premium}: the return spread between small and large market-capitalization stocks.
	\item \textbf{Value risk premium}: the return spread between value stocks (high book-to-market) and growth stocks (low book-to-market).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Excess Returns}
\textbf{Excess returns}: the difference between an asset's return and the return on some reference rate or benchmark.
\begin{equation}
	\dot{r}_i = \frac{1+r_i}{1+r_B} - 1 \quad \text{or quick approximation: } \dot{r}_i \approx r_i - r_B
	\label{eq:excess_return}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 33

\subsection{Real Returns}
\textbf{Real returns} are inflation-adjusted returns.
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{real}} = \frac{1+r_i}{1+\text{infl}} - 1 \quad \text{or quick approximation: } r_{\text{real}} \approx r_i - \text{infl}
	\label{eq:real_return}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 35

\subsection{Gross and Net Return}
\textbf{Gross return} = Total return - Transaction costs
\begin{itemize}
	\item[] An appropriate measure for evaluating and comparing the investment skill of managers.
	\item[] Excludes indirect expenses related to the management and administration of an investment.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Net return} = Gross return - Management fees - Administrative expenses 
\begin{itemize}
	\item[] The return the investor actually earns.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Pre-Tax and After-Tax Nominal Return}
\textbf{After-tax nominal return}:
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{net-tax}} = r_{\text{price}} \times (1 - \text{tax}_{\text{capital gains}}) + r_{\text{capital}} \times (1 - \text{tax}_{\text{capital distribution}})
	\label{eq:after_tax_return}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 39

Capital gains tax is a tax on a positive price return. Two forms of capital gains tax rate:
\begin{itemize}
	\item[] Short-term capital gains are levied on capital gains on short-term holdings.
	\item[] Long-term capital gains are levied on longer-term holdings and receive preferential tax treatment in a number of countries.
\end{itemize}
Forms of capital distribution tax rate:
\begin{itemize}
	\item[] Interest income is taxed as ordinary income in most countries. 
	\item[] Dividend income may be taxed as ordinary income, at a lower rate, or be exempt from taxes depending on the jurisdiction.
\end{itemize}

\begin{examtip}
In examinations, note the different tax rates applied to price returns (capital gains tax) versus income distributions (dividend or interest income tax). Always apply them to their respective components of the return formula.
\end{examtip}

\subsection{Leveraged Returns}
Using leverage to increase the total amount of investible assets is a common feature of investments, whether through borrowing or derivatives.
Leverage increases investible capital, magnifying both returns and risks.
\begin{equation}
	R_L = R_A + \frac{D}{E}(R_A - R_D)
	\label{eq:leveraged_return}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 1, Page 46
\begin{itemize}
	\item[] $R_L$ is the leveraged return (Return on Equity)
	\item[] $R_A$ is the return on total assets (unleveraged return)
	\item[] $R_D$ is the cost of debt (borrowing interest rate)
	\item[] $D/E$ is the debt-to-equity ratio.
\end{itemize}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Đòn bẩy là dao hai lưỡi. Khi suất sinh lời tài sản ($R_A$) lớn hơn chi phí đi vay ($R_D$), đòn bẩy phóng đại suất sinh lời vốn tự có ($R_L$). Nhưng nếu $R_A < R_D$, tỷ suất sinh lời của chúng ta sẽ sụt giảm rất nhanh!}}
