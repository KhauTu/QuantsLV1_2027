\subsection{Types of Returns: Financial Assets}
The main financial assets are cash, equity, and debt, as well as hybrids that combine their characteristics.

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Khi chi trả cổ tức $X$, lý thuyết giá giảm ngay đúng bằng $X$, khiến phần sinh lời phân phối bù trừ Price Return trong ngắn hạn. Chúng ta đã bao giờ quan sát hiện tượng này tại sàn chứng khoán Việt Nam vào ngày giao dịch không hưởng quyền chưa?}}

Let's detail the characteristics across major financial assets:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Cash:} does not generate any return unless invested. In the short term, excess cash is typically placed in interest-bearing bank accounts or short-term debt instruments.
	\item \textbf{Equities:} generate returns from capital appreciation and discretionary dividends (variable capital distributions). Rapidly growing companies often choose not to distribute dividends and instead reinvest earnings to fuel growth.
	\item \textbf{Bonds (Debt):} represent contractual lending agreements that pay fixed interest coupons (fixed capital distributions). Shorter-term bonds are dominated by capital distributions, whereas longer-term bonds exhibit substantial price volatility due to market interest rate movements.
	\item \textbf{Hybrids:} preferred stock and convertible bonds blend fixed-income features of debt with the growth potential of equity.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Types of Returns: Financial Instruments}
Financial instruments are standardized legal agreements for financial assets (such as equity and bonds), as well as derivatives and securitized debt. The standardization facilitates public trading.
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Cash Instruments:} represent direct ownership claims on financial assets (e.g., actual shares of stock or actual corporate bonds).
	\item \textbf{Derivatives:} financial instruments whose value is derived from the performance of another underlying financial asset or indicator (e.g., stock price, interest rate, index, exchange rate). A call option is a typical example.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Types of Returns: Financial Indicators}
Financial indicators do not directly generate cash flows, but are critical to financial market operations. 
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Interest Rates:} traded through risk-free government bonds. Their level varies inversely with bond prices.
	\item \textbf{The Risk-Free Rate:} represented by short-term, risk-free government debt (e.g., US Treasury bills).
\end{itemize}

\begin{examtip}
Short-term discount instruments (like T-bills) do not pay regular coupons ($\text{Inc}_t = 0$). Instead, they sell at a discount to face value, and the return is earned purely from the price appreciation when the bond matures at par value.
\end{examtip}

Let's review the annualized risk-free rate calculation using a Treasury Bill:
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{period}} = \frac{P_1 - P_0 + \text{Inc}_t}{P_0} = \frac{\text{Face Value} - P_0}{P_0}
	\label{eq:period_return_tbill}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 2, Page 63

To annualize this period return for a T-bill with $90$ days to maturity using $365$ days in a year:
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{annual}} = (1 + r_{\text{period}})^{\frac{365}{90}} - 1
	\label{eq:annual_return_tbill}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 2, Page 63

Consider a 90-day US Treasury Bill with a face value of USD 1,000 purchased at USD 990.
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Period return calculation:}
	      \[ r_{\text{period}} = \frac{1000 - 990}{990} \approx 1.01\% \]
	\item \textbf{Annualized return calculation (using 365 days):}
	      \[ r_{\text{annual}} = (1 + 0.0101)^{\frac{365}{90}} - 1 \approx 4.16\% \]
\end{itemize}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Thực tế: So sánh lãi suất năm thực tế ($4.16\%$ ghép lãi) và lãi suất đơn ($1.01\% \times \frac{365}{90} \approx 4.10\%$). Trong phòng thi, chúng ta hãy luôn ưu tiên phương pháp ghép lãi lũy thừa.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Foreign Exchange (FX) Rates:} represent the value of one currency expressed in terms of another currency.
\end{itemize}

When investing internationally, the home-currency return ($r_{\text{home}}$) depends on both the investment return in foreign currency ($r_{\text{foreign}}$) and the currency appreciation/depreciation rate ($r_{\text{FX}}$):
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{home}} = (1 + r_{\text{foreign}})(1 + r_{\text{FX}}) - 1 \approx r_{\text{foreign}} + r_{\text{FX}}
	\label{eq:foreign_exchange_adjust_m2}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 2, Page 65

Where:
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{FX}} = \frac{\text{Ending FX Rate} - \text{Beginning FX Rate}}{\text{Beginning FX Rate}}
	\label{eq:fx_rate_return_m2}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 2, Page 64

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Example: Foreign Exchange FX Adjustment on Returns},
	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,
	breakable
]
A US dollar-based investor purchases a bond denominated in Euros (EUR).
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Foreign asset return:} $r_{\text{foreign}} = -2.91\%$.
	\item \textbf{FX rate change (EUR to USD):} $r_{\text{FX}} = -6.90\%$ (the Euro depreciated from 1.2216 to 1.1373 USD/EUR).
	\item \textbf{Home-currency return calculation:}
	      \[ r_{\text{home}} = (1 - 0.0291)(1 - 0.0690) - 1 = -9.61\% \]
\end{itemize}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Thực tế: Tác động kép từ trái phiếu giảm giá và EUR mất giá so với USD, khuếch đại khoản lỗ bằng USD từ $-2.91\%$ thành $-9.61\%$.}}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Market Indexes:} reflect the aggregate value of a basket of assets (e.g., S\&P 500, OMX Copenhagen 25 Index) to indicate market trends.
\end{itemize}

To evaluate and compare performance, we look at the arithmetic and geometric averages of index returns:
\begin{equation}
	\bar{r}_{\text{arithmetic}} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}r_t
	\label{eq:arith_average_m2}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 2, Page 67

\begin{equation}
	\bar{r}_{\text{geometric}} = \left[ \prod_{t=1}^{T}(1+r_t) \right]^{\frac{1}{T}} - 1
	\label{eq:geom_average_m2}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 2, Page 67

\subsection{Historical Long-Term Asset Returns}
Based on historical data for selected major US asset classes between 1979 and 2023 (large-cap stocks, small-cap stocks, corporate bonds, government bonds, and Treasury bills), several trends emerge:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Equities Outperform Bonds:} driven primarily by capital appreciation, equities deliver higher long-term average returns than fixed-income indexes.
	\item \textbf{Small-Cap Premium:} small-capitalization stocks have periodically outperformed large-capitalization stocks (size premium), though this premium varies over time and can occasionally be negative.
	\item \textbf{Corporate vs. Government Debt:} corporate bonds offer higher coupon yields on paper, but government bonds can occasionally match or exceed corporate bond performance over specific horizons due to the impact of default losses.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Long-Term Asset Return Components}
The relative contribution of capital appreciation and capital distributions has evolved significantly:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Equities:} dividend yields have trended downward over time, particularly following a 1982 regulatory change that established a safe harbor for share buybacks. Companies increasingly repurchase shares to return cash to shareholders, which reduces share count, increases stock value, and provides tax advantages (capital gains tax is generally lower than dividend tax).
	\item \textbf{Government Bonds:} income return (coupon payments) remains the dominant driver of total returns, historically accounting for approximately 85\% of total government bond returns. Capital appreciation plays a minor role, and bond prices suffer capital losses during periods of rising interest rates (such as the early 2020s).
\end{itemize}

\textcolor{blue}{

\section{RISKS AND RETURNS}

\textcolor{blue}{
	\textit{Chúng ta cùng rà soát định nghĩa, cách tính suất sinh lời danh mục, phân loại rủi ro (hệ thống, phi hệ thống) và nguyên lý đánh đổi rủi ro - lợi nhuận (Risk-Return Trade-Off).}}

Risk represents the uncertainty of investment returns and can be categorized into macro and micro risks:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Systematic Risk (Macro Risks):} broad, economy-wide risks that affect all financial assets (e.g., inflation risk, market interest rate risk, and market risk). Systematic risk cannot be diversified away.
	\item \textbf{Unsystematic Risk (Micro Risks):} risks specific to a particular issuer or sector (e.g., credit/default risk, business risk, financial leverage, sector/industry risk). Unsystematic risk can be mitigated through diversification.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Portfolio Return Calculation}
The expected return of a portfolio is the weighted average of the returns of the individual assets in the portfolio:
\begin{equation}
	r_p = \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i r_i = w_1 r_1 + w_2 r_2 + \dots + w_N r_N
	\label{eq:portfolio_return_m2}
\end{equation}
% Reference: CFA Curriculum 2027, Volume 1, Module 2, Page 75

Where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item[] $w_i$ is the weight of asset $i$ in the portfolio (where $\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i = 1$).
	\item[] $r_i$ is the rate of return of asset $i$.
\end{itemize}
