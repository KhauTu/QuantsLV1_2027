\section{Calculating Portfolio Statistics}
\label{sec:Calculating Portfolio Statistics}

Portfolio statistics, such as expected returns, standard deviations, and correlations, are fundamental for effective portfolio management and asset allocation. These statistics can be historical (factoring in past returns) or forward-looking (using model-based forecasts).

\subsection{Returns of a Portfolio}
\label{subsec:Returns of a Portfolio}

The return of a portfolio is the weighted average of the returns of its individual assets. Historical portfolio returns are calculated by summing the products of the beginning-of-period asset weights and their corresponding realized returns:
\begin{equation}
	R_{P,t} = \sum_{i=1}^N w_{i,t-1} R_{i,t}
	\label{eq:portfolio_return_single_m8}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of assets, $w_{i,t-1}$ is the weight of asset $i$ at the end of period $t-1$ (beginning of period $t$), and $R_{i,t}$ is the realized return of asset $i$ in period $t$, defined as:
\begin{equation}
	R_{i,t} = \frac{P_{i,t} - P_{i,t-1} + \text{Div}_{i,t}}{P_{i,t-1}}
	\label{eq:asset_realized_return_m8}
\end{equation}

To evaluate returns over multiple periods from $t=1$ to $T$, if the weights are held constant at their initial values $w_{i,0}$ (which implies continuous rebalancing), the aggregate portfolio return is:
\begin{equation}
	R_{P,T} = \sum_{i=1}^N w_{i,0} R_{i,T}
	\label{eq:portfolio_cumulative_no_drift_m8}
\end{equation}
where $R_{i,T}$ is the cumulative return of asset $i$ over the entire period $T$. 
The arithmetic average return of the portfolio across these periods is:
\begin{equation}
	\bar{R}_P = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T R_{P,t}
	\label{eq:portfolio_arithmetic_average_m8}
\end{equation}

When compounding returns over multiple periods without rebalancing, the asset weights will naturally shift as a result of varying asset performance. This phenomenon is known as \textbf{portfolio drift}. The cumulative return over $T$ periods with drifting weights is:
\begin{equation}
	R_{P,T} = \prod_{t=1}^T \left(1 + \sum_{i=1}^N w_{i,t-1} R_{i,t}\right) - 1
	\label{eq:portfolio_cumulative_drift_m8}
\end{equation}
The geometric average return, or geometric return, is defined as:
\begin{equation}
	\bar{R}_{g,P} = \left[ \prod_{t=1}^T (1 + R_{P,t}) \right]^{\frac{1}{T}} - 1 = (1 + R_{P,T})^{\frac{1}{T}} - 1
	\label{eq:portfolio_geometric_return_m8}
\end{equation}

\textcolor{blue}{\textit{Portfolio drift làm lệch tỷ trọng mục tiêu (style drift) do tài sản tăng trưởng không đều. Chúng ta cần đặt câu hỏi: Nếu không tái cân bằng (rebalancing) định kỳ, rủi ro danh mục sẽ thay đổi thế nào? Có tiệm cận rủi ro của tài sản sinh lời cao nhất?}}

\subsection{Variance and Standard Deviation of Portfolio Returns}
\label{subsec:Variance and Standard Deviation}

The risk of a portfolio is measured by its variance and standard deviation. The variance of portfolio returns ($\sigma_P^2$) incorporates both individual asset variances and the covariances between all asset pairs:
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_P^2 = \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N w_i w_j \sigma_{i,j}
	\label{eq:portfolio_variance_sum_m8}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{i,j}$ represents the covariance between asset $i$ and asset $j$. Note that $\sigma_{i,i}$ is the variance of asset $i$ ($\sigma_i^2$).
Standard deviation ($\sigma_P$) is the square root of variance:
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_P = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N w_i w_j \sigma_{i,j}}
	\label{eq:portfolio_std_sum_m8}
\end{equation}

If covariances are modeled as random variables, the expected variance of portfolio returns is:
\begin{equation}
	E[\sigma_P^2] = \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N w_i w_j E[\sigma_{i,j}]
	\label{eq:portfolio_expected_variance_m8}
\end{equation}
Covariance is linked to the correlation coefficient ($\rho_{i,j}$) by:
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_{i,j} = \rho_{i,j} \sigma_i \sigma_j
	\label{eq:covariance_correlation_relation_m8}
\end{equation}
Due to the non-linear interaction of volatility and correlation, the expected covariance $E[\sigma_{i,j}] \neq E[\rho_{i,j}] \times E[\sigma_i] \times E[\sigma_j]$ in the presence of joint dependencies.

For a two-asset portfolio, the portfolio variance formula is:
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_P^2 = w_1^2 \sigma_1^2 + w_2^2 \sigma_2^2 + 2 w_1 w_2 \sigma_{1,2} = w_1^2 \sigma_1^2 + w_2^2 \sigma_2^2 + 2 w_1 w_2 \rho_{1,2} \sigma_1 \sigma_2
	\label{eq:two_asset_variance_m8}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Matrix Notation of Portfolio Risk}

For a portfolio with $N$ assets, the number of unique covariances that must be computed is:
\begin{equation}
	\text{Unique Covariances} = \frac{N(N-1)}{2}
	\label{eq:unique_covariances_m8}
\end{equation}
Along with the $N$ individual variances, the total parameters needed for an $N$-asset portfolio is $\frac{N(N+1)}{2}$. For example, a 100-security portfolio requires 4,950 unique covariances.

This structure is organized into the symmetric \textbf{variance-covariance matrix} $\mathbf{V}$:
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{V} = \begin{bmatrix}
		\sigma_1^2 & \sigma_{1,2} & \sigma_{1,3} & \cdots & \sigma_{1,N} \\
		\sigma_{2,1} & \sigma_2^2 & \sigma_{2,3} & \cdots & \sigma_{2,N} \\
		\sigma_{3,1} & \sigma_{3,2} & \sigma_3^2 & \cdots & \sigma_{3,N} \\
		\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
		\sigma_{N,1} & \sigma_{N,2} & \sigma_{N,3} & \cdots & \sigma_N^2
	\end{bmatrix}
	\label{eq:covariance_matrix_m8}
\end{equation}
The matching normalized matrix is the \textbf{correlation matrix} $\boldsymbol{\rho}$:
\begin{equation}
	\boldsymbol{\rho} = \begin{bmatrix}
		1 & \rho_{1,2} & \rho_{1,3} & \cdots & \rho_{1,N} \\
		\rho_{2,1} & 1 & \rho_{2,3} & \cdots & \rho_{2,N} \\
		\rho_{3,1} & \rho_{3,2} & 1 & \cdots & \rho_{3,N} \\
		\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
		\rho_{N,1} & \rho_{N,2} & \rho_{N,3} & \cdots & 1
	\end{bmatrix}
	\label{eq:correlation_matrix_m8}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Expected Return and Variance under Economic Scenarios}
\label{subsec:Scenarios}

Expected returns and covariances can be derived from joint probabilities over $M$ economic scenarios. Under scenario $M$ occurring with probability $p_M$, the portfolio return is:
\begin{equation}
	R_{P,M} = \sum_{i=1}^N w_i R_{i,M}
	\label{eq:scenario_portfolio_return_m8}
\end{equation}
The expected portfolio return over all scenarios is:
\begin{equation}
	E[R_P] = \sum_{M=1}^M p_M R_{P,M} = \sum_{M=1}^M p_M \left( \sum_{i=1}^N w_i R_{i,M} \right)
	\label{eq:expected_return_scenarios_m8}
\end{equation}
The portfolio variance is the weighted sum of squared deviations from the expected return:
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_P^2 = \sum_{M=1}^M p_M \left( R_{P,M} - E[R_P] \right)^2
	\label{eq:variance_scenarios_m8}
\end{equation}

\subsection{The Benefits of Diversification}
\label{subsec:The Benefits of Diversification}

The power of diversification lies in combining assets that are not perfectly positively correlated to reduce overall portfolio risk. 
Consider an equally weighted portfolio ($w_i = 1/N$) where all assets have the same variance $\sigma^2$ and the average correlation coefficient is $\bar{\rho}$. The portfolio variance is:
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_P^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{N} + \left(1 - \frac{1}{N}\right) \bar{\rho} \sigma^2
	\label{eq:equal_weighted_variance_m8}
\end{equation}

As the number of assets $N \to \infty$:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The contribution of the individual asset variance, $\frac{\sigma^2}{N}$, converges to $0$.
	\item The multiplier $\left(1 - \frac{1}{N}\right)$ approaches $1$.
\end{itemize}
This leaves only the average covariance term to determine the portfolio's risk:
\begin{equation}
	\lim_{N \to \infty} \sigma_P^2 = \bar{\rho} \sigma^2 = \text{Average Covariance}
	\label{eq:limit_variance_m8}
\end{equation}

\textcolor{blue}{\textit{Rủi ro danh mục đa dạng hóa cao chỉ phụ thuộc covariance trung bình giữa các tài sản (rủi ro hệ thống) chứ không phụ thuộc rủi ro riêng lẻ (phi hệ thống). Chúng ta hãy tự hỏi: Tại sao thị trường chỉ bù đắp lợi nhuận cho rủi ro hệ thống mà không bù đắp rủi ro phi hệ thống?}}

\section{The Minimum-Variance Portfolio and the Efficient Frontier}
\label{sec:The Minimum-Variance Portfolio and the Efficient Frontier}

Portfolio optimization involves finding the optimal weights of assets to minimize risk for a given target return, or maximize expected return for a given risk level.

\subsection{The Feasible Set and Efficient Frontier}
\label{subsec:The Feasible Set and Efficient Frontier}

The \textbf{feasible set} (or investment opportunity set) represents all possible portfolios that can be constructed from a given group of assets. When there are multiple risky assets, the feasible set forms a cloud of portfolios in risk-return space.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{002_figs/008_Module_8/exhibit_13.png}
	\caption{The Cloud of Feasible Portfolios in Risk-Return Space}
	\label{fig:Feasible_Cloud}
\end{figure}

The boundary of this cloud on the upper-left represents the optimal trade-off between risk and return:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The \textbf{Minimum-Variance Frontier} is the boundary that minimizes variance for each level of expected return.
	\item The \textbf{Global Minimum-Variance Portfolio} is the single portfolio on the frontier that has the lowest possible risk.
	\item The \textbf{Markowitz Efficient Frontier} is the portion of the minimum-variance frontier that lies above the Global Minimum-Variance portfolio. Risk-averse, utility-maximizing investors will only choose portfolios on this segment.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{002_figs/008_Module_8/exhibit_15.png}
	\caption{The Minimum-Variance Frontier and the Efficient Frontier}
	\label{fig:Efficient_Frontier}
\end{figure}

Mathematically, the optimization problem is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
	\text{Minimize } \sigma_P^2 = \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N w_i w_j \sigma_{i,j} \quad \text{subject to } \sum_{i=1}^N w_i = 1, \quad w_i \ge 0, \quad E[R_P] = k
	\label{eq:optimization_sum_m8}
\end{equation}
where $k$ is the target expected return. Under matrix form:
\begin{equation}
	\text{Minimize } \sigma_P^2 = \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{V} \mathbf{w} \quad \text{subject to } \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{1} = 1, \quad \mathbf{w}^T \boldsymbol{\mu} = k, \quad \mathbf{w} \ge \mathbf{0}
	\label{eq:optimization_matrix_m8}
\end{equation}
The constraint $w_i \ge 0$ represents the \textbf{no-short-selling} constraint.

\subsection{Derivation of the Minimum-Variance Portfolio Weights}
\label{subsec:Derivation of MVP}

For a two-asset portfolio, we can find the weights that minimize variance without complex optimization. Let $w_A$ be the weight of Asset A and $w_B = 1 - w_A$ be the weight of Asset B.
\[
\sigma_P^2 = w_A^2 \sigma_A^2 + (1 - w_A)^2 \sigma_B^2 + 2 w_A (1 - w_A) \sigma_{A,B}
\]
Taking the first derivative of $\sigma_P^2$ with respect to $w_A$ and setting it equal to zero:
\[
\frac{d\sigma_P^2}{dw_A} = 2 w_A \sigma_A^2 - 2(1 - w_A) \sigma_B^2 + 2(1 - 2w_A) \sigma_{A,B} = 0
\]
\[
w_A (\sigma_A^2 + \sigma_B^2 - 2\sigma_{A,B}) - \sigma_B^2 + \sigma_{A,B} = 0
\]
Solving for the optimal weight $w_A^*$ gives the global minimum-variance weight:
\begin{equation}
	w_A^* = \frac{\sigma_B^2 - \sigma_{A,B}}{\sigma_A^2 + \sigma_B^2 - 2\sigma_{A,B}}
	\label{eq:MVP_weight_A_m8}
\end{equation}
Correspondingly, the weight for Asset B is:
\begin{equation}
	w_B^* = 1 - w_A^* = \frac{\sigma_A^2 - \sigma_{A,B}}{\sigma_A^2 + \sigma_B^2 - 2\sigma_{A,B}}
	\label{eq:MVP_weight_B_m8}
\end{equation}

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,
	boxrule=0.5pt,
	arc=3pt,
	title=\textbf{Example 11: Calculating Minimum-Variance Weights},
	coltitle=black,

	before skip=6pt,
	after skip=6pt,

]

Calculate the weights, return, and standard deviation of the global minimum-variance portfolio.

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Tỷ trọng tối ưu (Optimal Weights) theo Phương trình~\ref{eq:MVP_weight_A_m8}:}
	      \[
	      w_A^* = \frac{0.1096 - 0.0107}{0.0263 + 0.1096 - 2(0.0107)} = \frac{0.0989}{0.1145} \approx 86.4\%
	      \]
	      Tỷ trọng của tài sản B:
	      \[
	      w_B^* = 1 - 0.864 = 13.6\%
	      \]
	\item \textbf{Tỷ suất sinh lời kỳ vọng (Expected Return):} $E[R_P] = 0.864(9.93\%) + 0.136(18.20\%) = 11.05\%$.
	\item \textbf{Rủi ro danh mục / Độ lệch chuẩn (Portfolio Risk):}
	      \[
	      \sigma_P = \sqrt{(0.864^2 \times 0.0263) + (0.136^2 \times 0.1096) + 2(0.864)(0.136)(0.0107)}
	      \]
	      \[
	      \sigma_P = \sqrt{0.01963 + 0.00203 + 0.00251} = \sqrt{0.02417} \approx 15.55\%
	      \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Optimal Portfolio Selection}
\label{sec:Optimal Portfolio Selection}

Optimal portfolio selection balances expected returns with risk, matching the opportunity set against an investor's personal preferences.

\subsection{Investor Utility and Indifference Curves}
\label{subsec:Investor Utility}

The utility function represents an investor's satisfaction or ranking of portfolios based on risk and expected return. For a risk-averse investor, utility is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
	U = E[R] - \frac{1}{2} A \sigma^2
	\label{eq:utility_function_m8}
\end{equation}
where $E[R]$ is expected return, $\sigma^2$ is variance of returns, and $A$ is the \textbf{coefficient of risk aversion}.
\begin{itemize}
	\item $A > 0$: Risk-averse investor. Utility decreases with higher risk.
	\item $A = 0$: Risk-neutral investor. Utility depends solely on expected return.
	\item $A < 0$: Risk-seeking investor. Utility increases with risk.
\end{itemize}

An \textbf{indifference curve} maps the combinations of risk and expected return that yield the same level of utility.

\centering

\label{tab:risk_preferences_indifference_curves_m8}

	\toprule
	\textbf{Preference} & \textbf{Aversion ($A$)} & \textbf{Indifference Slope} & \textbf{Utility Curve Shape} \\
	\midrule
	\textbf{Risk-seeking} & $A < 0$ & Downward/Concave & Convex utility in wealth \\
	\midrule
	\textbf{Risk-neutral} & $A = 0$ & Flat/Horizontal & Linear utility in expected return \\
	\midrule
	\textbf{Risk-averse} & $A > 0$ & Upward/Convex & Concave utility in wealth \\
	\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{The Capital Allocation Line (CAL) and Sharpe Ratio}
\label{subsec:CAL}

When a risk-free asset (with return $R_f$ and standard deviation $\sigma_f = 0$) is introduced, investors can combine it with a risky portfolio $P$.
The expected return of the combined portfolio is:
\begin{equation}
	E[R_c] = w_f R_f + (1 - w_f) E[R_P]
	\label{eq:CAL_expected_return_m8}
\end{equation}
The standard deviation is:
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_c = (1 - w_f) \sigma_P \implies (1 - w_f) = \frac{\sigma_c}{\sigma_P}
	\label{eq:CAL_std_m8}
\end{equation}
Substituting $(1 - w_f)$ into the expected return equation yields the \textbf{Capital Allocation Line (CAL)}:
\begin{equation}
	E[R_c] = R_f + \left( \frac{E[R_P] - R_f}{\sigma_P} \right) \sigma_c
	\label{eq:CAL_equation_m8}
\end{equation}

The slope of the CAL is the \textbf{Sharpe ratio (SR)} of the risky portfolio:
\begin{equation}
	\text{Sharpe Ratio} = \frac{E[R_P] - R_f}{\sigma_P}
	\label{eq:sharpe_ratio_m8}
\end{equation}
The optimal risky portfolio (tangency portfolio) is the point on the efficient frontier that maximizes the Sharpe ratio, creating the steepest possible CAL.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{002_figs/008_Module_8/exhibit_24.png}
	\caption{Optimal Portfolio Selection via Tangency between Indifference Curves and CAL}
	\label{fig:Optimal_Selection_CAL}
\end{figure}

The optimal portfolio for a specific investor lies at the point of tangency between their highest attainable indifference curve and the CAL:
\begin{itemize}
	\item More risk-averse investors select portfolios closer to the risk-free asset (left on the CAL).
	\item Less risk-averse investors select portfolios closer to or beyond the tangency portfolio $P$, utilizing leverage (borrowing at the risk-free rate) to buy more of the risky portfolio.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Capital Market Line (CML)}
\label{subsec:CML}

If all investors share identical assumptions and hold mean-variance efficient portfolios, the optimal risky portfolio is the \textbf{Market Portfolio} ($M$). 
The Market Portfolio theoretically contains all risky assets in the universe, weighted by their market values.
The Capital Allocation Line using the Market Portfolio as the risky portfolio is called the \textbf{Capital Market Line (CML)}:
\begin{equation}
	E[R_P] = R_f + \left( \frac{E[R_M] - R_f}{\sigma_M} \right) \sigma_P
	\label{eq:CML_equation_m8}
\end{equation}
where $E[R_M]$ and $\sigma_M$ are the expected return and standard deviation of the market portfolio.

According to the \textbf{mutual fund theorem}, all rational investors can achieve optimal asset allocation by holding only two assets: the risk-free asset and the market portfolio.

\end{figure}

\subsection{The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) and Beta}
\label{subsec:CAPM}

Total risk consists of two parts:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Unsystematic Risk} (diversifiable / idiosyncratic risk): Unique to a specific company or sector. Can be eliminated in a well-diversified portfolio.
	\item \textbf{Systematic Risk} (undiversifiable / market risk): Driven by macroeconomic factors that affect all assets. Cannot be diversified away.
\end{itemize}
Since investors can eliminate unsystematic risk at no cost through diversification, they are not compensated for bearing it. The expected return of an asset depends solely on its systematic risk.

Systematic risk is measured by \textbf{Beta} ($\beta$), which quantifies the sensitivity of an asset's returns to movements in the market portfolio:
\begin{equation}
	\beta_i = \frac{\sigma_{i,M}}{\sigma_M^2} = \frac{\rho_{i,M} \sigma_i \sigma_M}{\sigma_M^2} = \rho_{i,M} \frac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_M}
	\label{eq:beta_definition_m8}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{i,M}$ is the covariance between asset $i$ and the market, and $\sigma_M^2$ is the variance of the market portfolio.
\begin{itemize}
	\item $\beta = 1.0$: Asset has the same systematic risk as the market.
	\item $\beta > 1.0$: Asset is more volatile than the market (aggressive asset).
	\item $\beta < 1.0$: Asset is less volatile than the market (defensive asset).
\end{itemize}

The \textbf{Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)} states that the expected return of asset $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
	E[R_i] = R_f + \beta_i \left( E[R_M] - R_f \right)
	\label{eq:CAPM_equation_m8}
\end{equation}
where $\left( E[R_M] - R_f \right)$ is the \textbf{market risk premium (MRP)}.

The graphical representation of CAPM is the \textbf{Security Market Line (SML)}, which plots expected return against Beta. In contrast to the CML (which uses total risk / standard deviation and applies only to efficient portfolios), the SML uses systematic risk / Beta and applies to \textbf{all} assets, whether efficient or individual.
\begin{equation}
	E[R_i] = R_f + \left(\frac{E[R_M] - R_f}{\sigma_M^2}\right) \sigma_{i,M}
	\label{eq:SML_covariance_form_m8}
\end{equation}

\textcolor{blue}{\textit{CML dùng tổng rủi ro ($\sigma$) cho danh mục hiệu quả. SML (CAPM) dùng rủi ro hệ thống ($\beta$) cho mọi tài sản. Chúng ta hãy thảo luận: Điểm khác biệt cốt lõi về rủi ro giữa CML và SML là gì? Tại sao cổ phiếu đơn lẻ có độ lệch chuẩn lớn không nằm trên CML nhưng vẫn nằm trên SML?}}

	coltitle=black,
	colbacktitle=black!5!white,
	before skip=6pt,

	breakable

Consider: $R_f = 2.0\%$, $E[R_M] = 8.0\%$, $\sigma_M = 15.0\%$, and an investor's optimal portfolio has standard deviation $\sigma_P = 10.0\%$.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Calculate the expected return of the portfolio using CML.
	\item Calculate the Beta of the portfolio.
	\item Calculate the expected return using CAPM and confirm it matches the CML.
	\item Calculate the Sharpe ratio of the market portfolio.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{CML expected return:}
	      \[
	      E[R_P] = R_f + \left(\frac{E[R_M] - R_f}{\sigma_M}\right) \sigma_P = 2\% + \left(\frac{8\% - 2\%}{15\%}\right)(10\%) = 2\% + 0.4(10\%) = 6\%
	      \]
	\item \textbf{Beta:} For an efficient portfolio on the CML, the correlation with the market is $\rho = 1.0$:
	      \[
	      \beta_P = \frac{\sigma_P}{\sigma_M} = \frac{10\%}{15\%} \approx 0.6667
	      \]
	\item \textbf{CAPM return:}
	      \[
	      E[R_P] = R_f + \beta_P (E[R_M] - R_f) = 2\% + 0.6667(8\% - 2\%) = 2\% + 4\% = 6\%
	      \]
	      Kết quả tính toán khớp nhau hoàn toàn, xác nhận tính nhất quán giữa CML và CAPM đối với các danh mục đầu tư hiệu quả.
	\item \textbf{Sharpe ratio:} $\text{SR}_M = \frac{8\% - 2\%}{15\%} = 0.40$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Estimating Beta using Linear Regression}

In practice, Beta is estimated using ordinary least squares (OLS) linear regression of the asset's excess returns against the market's excess returns. This is known as the \textbf{Market Model}:
\begin{equation}
	R_{i,t} - R_{f,t} = \alpha_i + \beta_i \left( R_{M,t} - R_{f,t} \right) + \epsilon_{i,t}
	\label{eq:market_model_m8}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_i$ is the intercept, $\beta_i$ is the slope, and $\epsilon_{i,t}$ is the error term. Under CAPM, the expected alpha ($\alpha_i$) should be exactly 0 in equilibrium.

\begin{tcolorbox}[
	colback=gray!5!white,
	colframe=black!15!white,

An analyst regresses monthly returns of LVMH Moët Hennessy Louis Vuitton against the CAC 40 index over 3 years ($36$ data points). The Bloomberg regression output shows:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Regression line: $y = 0.939 x + 1.318$ ($\beta = 0.939$, $\alpha = 1.318\%$)
	\item Correlation coefficient $R = 0.850$ ($R^2 = 0.722$)
	\item Standard error of Beta: $0.101$
\end{itemize}
The expected market return is $9.577\%$ and the risk-free rate is $2.560\%$. Calculate LVMH's expected return.

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Giải thích ý nghĩa Beta:} Chỉ số Beta bằng $0.939$ cho thấy cổ phiếu LVMH có rủi ro hệ thống thấp hơn một chút so với mức trung bình của thị trường chứng khoán Pháp (CAC 40 index).
	\item \textbf{Tỷ suất sinh lời kỳ vọng (CAPM):}
	      \[
	      E[R_{\text{LVMH}}] = R_f + \beta (E[R_M] - R_f) = 2.56\% + 0.939(9.577\% - 2.56\%)
	      \]
	      \[
	      E[R_{\text{LVMH}}] = 2.56\% + 0.939(7.017\%) = 2.56\% + 6.589\% \approx 9.15\%
	      \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Limitations of Portfolio Theory and CAPM}
\label{subsec:Limitations}

Several assumptions underlying Markowitz optimization and CAPM do not hold in real-world markets:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Distributional Assumptions:} Financial returns are rarely normally distributed, exhibiting fat tails (high kurtosis) and skewness.
	\item \textbf{Time-Varying Volatility and Correlation:} Variances and correlations are not constant but fluctuate, especially rising during systemic market crises.
	\item \textbf{Market Inefficiencies:} Information asymmetries exist, leading to mispricings and non-zero alphas.
	\item \textbf{Estimation Errors:} Small errors in the expected return and covariance inputs can result in dramatically different and highly concentrated optimal weights (input sensitivity).
	\item \textbf{Historical limits:} Historical relationships may not persist in the future.
	\item \textbf{Transaction Costs and Taxes:} Real-world investors must pay brokerage fees, spreads, and taxes. Capital distributions (dividends) and capital gains face differing tax structures, which reduces the after-tax efficient frontier.
\end{itemize}
