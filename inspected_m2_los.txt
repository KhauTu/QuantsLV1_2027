The correct response is B: Debt instruments typically provide returns in the

Alternative C is incorrect: Variable capital distributions are more commonly

3. F or international bond investments, foreign exchange rates:
A. determine the bond’s coupon rate.
B. have no impact on the bond’s returns.
C. affect the investor’s home currency return.
Solution
The correct answer is C: Changes in exchange rates influence the realized 
returns of international investments when converting back to the investor’s 
home currency. As the relative value of the two currencies changes, the 
return the investor earns is also impacted by these changes. As the domestic 
currency depreciates against the foreign currency, the returns increase, and 
vice versa.
Alternative A is incorrect: The bond’s coupon rate is predetermined and not 
directly affected by foreign exchange rates.
Alternative B is incorrect: Foreign exchange rates can have a significant 
impact on the returns of international investments.
4. D oes the variability of annual returns relate to investment risk?
A. No, there is no relationship.
B. Yes, greater variability indicates lower risk.
C. Yes, greater variability indicates higher risk.
Solution
The correct answer is C: The range of annual returns is a measure of vari-
ability and volatility. A wider range in annual returns signifies greater fluctu-
ations in value, implying higher risk and the possibility of greater rewards or 
losses.
Alternative A is incorrect: There is a relationship between annual returns 
and investment risk.
Alternative B is incorrect: A greater variability does not indicate lower risk, 
it indicates the opposite.

TYPES OF RETURNS FOR FINANCIAL ASSETS, 
INSTRUMENTS, AND INDICATORS
calculate, compare, and interpret different types of returns for 
financial assets, instruments, and indicators
Investors can earn returns from two main sources: uncertain price returns, such as 
price appreciation and price depreciation; and capital distribution returns, which can 
either be fixed or variable. These different returns influence the valuation of financial 
assets and how they are included in investment portfolios.
Types of Returns
The proportion of total return coming from each source varies depending on the 
specific type of financial asset or instrument:
 ■ Price return  is generally uncertain and variable, reflects changes in a finan -
cial asset’s price, and refers to changes in the asset price that are indepen -
dent of the capital income distributions. For equities, it reflects the compa -
ny’s performance, market sentiment, economic and fiscal conditions, and 
other factors. For bonds, it usually reflects the level of interest rates and the 
issuer’s financial health. Many financial assets and instruments generate 
returns primarily through price changes; thus, variability in price changes 
tends to be a strong focus of risk analysis.
 ■ Fixed capital distribution return  is the most common type of return for 
debt. Typically, this rate of return remains unchanged over the investment 
period. The realized and the actual value may vary, for example, in the case 
of issuer default.
 ■ Variable capital distribution return  is the most common type of return 
for equity in the form of dividends and for certain types of debt where 
the coupon rates and payments are variable. Both the rate of return and 
income vary over time, with dividends and floating-rate bonds being typical 
examples.

Types of Returns: Financial Assets
The main financial assets are cash, equity, and debt. There are also hybrids that com -
bine the characteristics of equity and debt.
 ■ Cash, essentially coins, bank notes, currency, and non-interest-bearing bank 
deposits, does not generate any return unless invested. Having cash available 
is vital in finance, investment, and portfolio management; every investment 
will eventually find itself converted back into cash. When cash is invested 
over the short-term, the typical choice is short-term bonds or interest-bear -
ing bank accounts because they earn capital distributions. Long-term invest -
ments include equities, debt, and alternative assets. Those investments earn 
price returns, capital distributions, or typically both.
 ■ Equities, also called stocks, shares, or ownership interests, generate return 
from price appreciation and capital distributions in the form of dividends.

 ● Price return . Equity values are determined by a complex interplay of 
company-specific fundamental factors, broader economic indicators, 
market sentiment, and global events, which will be covered in greater 
detail later in the curriculum. As these factors change, equity prices 
and returns change as well. Typically, most returns come from capital 
appreciation.
 ● Capital distributions . Investors who invest capital in corporations may 
receive discretionary capital distributions in the form of dividends or 
other cash payments. Investors receive these discretionary distribu -
tions on a quarterly, semiannual, or annual basis (and there may also be 
special one-off dividends tied to corporate actions). Some companies, 
particularly rapidly growing companies, may choose not to distribute 
dividends and instead reinvest profits to fuel growth. Other compa -
nies distribute a substantial portion of their earnings to shareholders. 
Consequently, investors can select companies that offer the type of 
return they desire.
